Statistical Alpha Signal Combination & Robust Backtesting in Long/Short Equity

Thirteen cross-sectional equity factors, run through a statistical gate on the S&P 100 (2020–2026), then combined into one signal. Every information coefficient is corrected for the autocorrelation that overlapping forward returns inject and carries a bootstrap confidence interval — a factor is kept only on robust evidence. The frozen result is a market-neutral, all-weather alpha that is profitable in both market regimes, presented here with the one structural weakness it cannot solve.

Four findings, in sequence

Each follows from the last: why the blend works, where it breaks, what would fix it, and the one adjustment that already helps.

Barbellwhy it works

Momentum and low-vol are regime mirror images

Sector-neutral momentum is the bull engine (+1.18 bull / −0.73 bear); the as-built defensive leg is the bear engine (+1.00 bear / −1.71 bull). Their near-zero IC correlation means they win on different days — the anticorrelation is exactly what makes the blend positive across both stable regimes.

The gapwhere it breaks

The transition defeats both legs at once

At the bear→bull turn both legs lose together (Sharpe ≈ −1.0). The combiner orients each factor by a slow trailing IC that still reflects the regime that just ended, so the book carries the wrong sign into the recovery — the classic momentum crash, and the source of the −16% drawdown. Daily IC has no short-term persistence, so the orientation can only move slowly.

Missing enginewhat would fix it

Reversal is the transition engine — but not statically

The short-term-reversal family alone earns Sharpe +2.23 through the turn. Yet it is full-sample dead, so a static combiner sizes it toward zero and the slow legs dominate the book; the edge survives in the ranking (transition IC −0.10 → −0.01) but dies in the sizing. Capturing it needs regime-conditional orientation, not one static weight vector.

The fixwhat already helps

A faster IC window shortens the orientation lag

Decaying the trailing-IC window (EWM, 126-day halflife) lets the sign re-orient sooner: the bear Sharpe lifts from 0.53 to 0.89 and the transition softens. It does not solve the turn — there is no fast IC signal to lock onto — but it is the strongest variant found, and the reason it is the frozen choice.

Every decision, and why

Triage 13 → 7 A positive-IC momentum cluster and a negative-IC risk cluster survive; six factors fail at every horizon tested.
Curate inputs SectorNeutralMomentum + LowVol — both studied factors, near-independent in daily IC (corr ≈ 0), so the pair diversifies rather than double-counts.
Choose combiner Signed IC-weighting and Lasso each flip the negative-IC low-vol leg to its profitable orientation; equal-weight cannot, and fails. ICWeighted with an EWM window is strongest.
Freeze horizon 31-day rebalance — the slow cadence where the defensive bear protection concentrates.
Verdict A frozen, in-sample all-weather alpha — with one open gap at the regime transition.

The statistics under the gate

The central correction is the one that makes overlapping-return significance honest; the rest are the metrics behind the tear sheet.

Show the estimators
Newey–West (HAC) t-statistic. Overlapping multi-day forward returns make adjacent IC observations serially correlated, which deflates the naive standard error and inflates the t-stat. The HAC correction widens the error to absorb that autocorrelation: $$t_{\text{NW}} = \frac{\bar{\text{IC}}}{\hat{\sigma}_{\text{NW}} / \sqrt{T}}, \qquad \hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{NW}} = \hat{\gamma}_0 + 2\sum_{j=1}^{L}\left(1 - \frac{j}{L+1}\right)\hat{\gamma}_j$$ with $L = h-1$ (horizon minus one) and $\hat{\gamma}_j$ the autocovariances. Without it, overlapping 21-day returns inflate t-statistics several-fold.
Block bootstrap 95% CI. Resamples the IC series in blocks of length $h$ to preserve serial dependence, takes the mean IC of each of $B = 10{,}000$ resamples, and reports the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles. A factor is retained only when this interval excludes zero.
Jensen's alpha & Sharpe. Annualized return unexplained by market beta, and excess return per unit of total volatility: $$\alpha = \big[(\bar{r}_s - r_f) - \beta(\bar{r}_b - r_f)\big]\,N, \qquad \text{SR} = \frac{\bar{r}-r_f}{\sigma}\sqrt{N}, \quad N=252.$$
Tail risk. Maximum drawdown is the largest peak-to-trough decline of the equity curve; CVaR is the expected loss in the worst $\alpha\%$ of days, $\text{CVaR}_\alpha = \mathbb{E}[\,r \mid r \le \text{VaR}_\alpha\,]$.

All metrics use simple returns on a dollar-neutral long/short book, annualized at 252 trading days, with 10 bps modeled per rebalance.
